Graph databases

Advantages of graph databases

  • High performance for querying chains of connections
  • Storing of data as nodes and edges introduces relationship-based thinking already in the early stages of data preparation and acquisition
  • Facilitates querying for data that is already structured in a way needed for network research

Clustering in bipartite graphs

Clustering Coefficient

  • Watts/Strogatz 1998: Collective dynamics of ‘small-world’ networks
  • Measure of cohesion; defines "cliquishness of a typical neighbourhood" (Watts/Strogatz 1998, 440)
  • Key concept: triadic closure (Simmel)
  • High Clustering Coefficient associated with, for example,
    • Dense (social) structures (observed in most real-world networks)
    • Localized closeness, redundancy and multiplicity of ties at the cost of effective longer connectivity (strength of weak ties)
    • Tendency towards homogeneity; processes of influencing and consolidation
    • Small-world properties (low network density, high clustering, average short path length)
  • Global and local clustering coefficient; for one or both modes

Clustering Coefficient

One-mode networks

→ Fraction between the number of observed triangles to all possible triangles in one network

Two-mode- and one-mode-data

6-cycles, stars and squares vs. triads and dyads

  • Problems of projections:
    • Information loss
    • Inflation of number of edges; higher clustering than in original bipartite graph
    • Unimodal patterns like dyads and triads can originate from more than one bipartite pattern

→ One-mode-measures have to be adapted or new measures be developed

Bimodal Clustering Coefficient

Zhang et al. 2008: Squares

  • Based on squares as basic/smallest possible cycle in bimodal networks, as triad is in unimodal networks
  • Clustering coefficient C4(i): fraction between number of existent squares and total number of possible squares
    • Squares: for a given node i the number of observed squares is given by the number of common neighbors among its neighbors
    • Possible Squares: given by the sum over each pair of neighbors of the sum between their degrees, after substracting the common node i
  • Based on definition by Lind et al. 2005, but adapted to bipartite networks
  • Not equivalent to concept of triadic closure, because only 2 nodes

Bimodal Clustering Coefficient

Opsahl 2013: 6-cycles and 4-paths

  • Based on 6-cycles as 2-mode representations of triads
  • For global clustering coefficient one node set must be designated as primary (responsible for tie generation; first and last nodes of 4-paths)
  • Local clustering coefficient undefined for nodes with two-mode degree < 2
  • Range between 0 and 1 as the numerator is a subset of the denominator

Bimodal Clustering Coefficient

6-cycles and 4-paths?

  • 6-cycles for M1-1: 8
  • 4-paths for M1-1: 6
  • → Clustering Coefficient: range not necessarily 0 - 1
  • 5-paths for M1-1: 8

Bimodal Clustering Coefficient

6-cycles and 5-paths

  • Graph-theoretical: more branches in 6-cycles than in 4-paths, if network strongly connected; 5-paths same amount of branches as 6-cycles → coefficient between 0 and 1
  • Network-theoretical: Analogous to 3-paths and squares, 5-paths give more information about nature of possible connections

→ Different approach to clustering coefficient with 6-cycles

Bimodal Clustering Coefficient

6-cycles and stars

Preliminary results use case:

  • Projection Objects: 30,90% of triads from 6-cycles, 69,10% from stars
  • Projection Graves: 74,53% of triads from 6-cycles, 25,47% from stars
